# Personal Software

The cultural tailwind behind HTML-as-artifact: the idea that LLMs let ordinary people make small, personal, often disposable software — and that an artifact is something you can build for a few people and never ship.

## The Lineage

- **Clay Shirky, "situated software"** (2004) — software "personal from its inception," not personalized after the fact. The pre-LLM origin.
- **Robin Sloan, "An app can be a home-cooked meal"** (Feb 2020) — *"When you liberate programming from the requirement to be professional and scalable, it becomes a different activity altogether."* His 2025 follow-up adds the value of *finishedness*: home-cooked apps "are allowed to just: be finished" and "won't change unless we want [them] to" — durability and sovereignty.
- **Maggie Appleton, "Home-Cooked Software and Barefoot Developers"** (Local-First Conf, 2024) — "language models will create a golden age of local, home-cooked software and barefoot developers": technically-savvy, community-embedded people stuck between end-users and pro developers, who never crossed the "command line wall."
- **[[product-engineering/Geoffrey Litt|Geoffrey Litt]]** — "one-off GUIs": "People use AI to create entire GUI applications just for performing a single specific task." And the argument against chat: *"Chat is an essentially limited interaction mode"* — which is the case for a manipulable *artifact* over a transcript. See [[product-engineering/Malleable Software]].

## Why It Matters

This movement legitimizes *small, personal, disposable, artifact-shaped software made by people who decide what to build* — precisely Archetype's user. A self-contained HTML file is the natural vessel for it: portable, runnable anywhere, and (per [[The Disposable Artifact]]) cheap to regenerate. The Ink & Switch warning is worth heeding, though: AI generation alone is "like bringing a talented sous chef to a food court" — it needs a malleable substrate to drop into.
