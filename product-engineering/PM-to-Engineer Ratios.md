# PM-to-Engineer Ratios

The quantitative version of [[PM–Engineer Convergence]]: how many engineers each product manager supports, and the thesis that AI compresses the ratio.

## The Historical Norm

The ~20-year rule of thumb traces to [[Marty Cagan]] (2007): **one PM per 6–10 engineers**. In practice:

- Google / Microsoft: ~1:6–9
- Meta: ~1:12 (heavy senior-IC reliance)
- [[Amazon]] two-pizza teams: ~1:6–8

The ratio came from "coding is the constraint" — engineers were the scarce, expensive resource, so one PM could direct many.

## The AI-Compression Argument

If AI removes the coding constraint, the PM (the decision-maker) becomes the new bottleneck, and the ratio compresses. Supporting data:

- Developers using Copilot completed coding tasks ~55% faster; "~46% of code is now AI-generated."
- A McKinsey study of 40 PMs found ~40% productivity gains — but concentrated in *artifact production* (PRDs, status updates), not discovery.
- A Dec 2025 survey of 1,750 product professionals found PM AI usage clusters in PRD writing (21.5%), mockups (19.8%), and communication (18.5%) — and just **1.1% in roadmap strategy.** AI helps PMs make *documents*, not *decisions*.

Because AI lifts dev velocity faster than it lifts product discovery, one CTO (Jeremy Freeman, Allstacks, Mar 2026) projects compression toward **1:3–4 near-term, potentially 1:1–2 within five years.**

## Caveat

The historical 1:6–10 figure is solid and widely corroborated. The "compresses to 1:1–2" endpoint is a *forecast*, not established data — and PM hiring is simultaneously at record highs ([[Will AI Replace Product Managers]]). The defensible claim: the ratio is under genuine downward pressure for AI-forward orgs, and the limit case is the [[The One-Person Company|one-person company]].
