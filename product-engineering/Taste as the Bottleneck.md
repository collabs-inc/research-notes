# Taste as the Bottleneck

When implementation is nearly free, *judgment* — knowing what to build, what good looks like, and when to stop — becomes the scarce, differentiating resource. This is the intellectual core of the [[The Product Engineer|product-engineer]] thesis and of Archetype.

## The Claim

- *"The time between 'I know what we should build' and 'here is a working prototype' has compressed from weeks to hours. The scarce resource isn't engineering capacity anymore. It's knowing what is actually worth building."*
- **[[Marty Cagan]]:** *"As the cost of product delivery continues to drop, the bottleneck and competitive advantage moves to product discovery. The hard part is rarely building; the hard part is discovering the right solution to build."*
- **Sam Altman** (Feb 2026): *"We believe the best research teams are built through context, taste and a real feel for where the field is headed next"* — framing taste as something AI struggles to replicate and a way non-technical people can contribute.
- **a16z** (Design Engineer Fellowship): *"AI is commoditizing execution — everyone can make stuff now. So the defensible value is shifting from exploration to context and taste-making."*
- **Sequoia** (AI Ascent 2025): "labour — human or AI — is vanishingly cheap; what becomes scarce is taste."

## Two Senses of "Taste"

It's worth separating two adjacent ideas:

- **Product judgment** — *what* to build and *whether it's worth building* (this note). The constraint moves from engineering capacity to deciding correctly.
- **[[Craft as a Moat|Craft]]** — *whether the thing is good*: polish, motion, the felt quality of the result.

Both appreciate as building commoditizes. Together they define the work that survives automation — and the open question the sources raise but don't settle: can taste itself be learned by AI?
