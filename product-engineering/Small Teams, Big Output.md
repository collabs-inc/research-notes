# Small Teams, Big Output

The organizing principle shared across nearly every [[Company Case Studies|company]] in this vault: keep teams small, give them full ownership, and let AI amplify per-person scope. What began as a cultural preference is becoming an AI-amplified economic frontier.

## The Pattern

- **[[PostHog]]:** "one-pizza teams" of 2–6 people, each owning a product area "like an early-stage startup." James Temperton: "More than this and you have a department, which is what we're trying to avoid."
- **[[Amazon]]:** two-pizza teams (<10), evolved into "single-threaded ownership" — one leader owning one thing across its full lifecycle.
- **[[Linear]]:** ephemeral project pods (~1 designer + 2 engineers) that disband on completion.
- **[[Ramp]]:** teams of ~5–10 engineers; hit $100M ARR with fewer than 5 PMs and ~50 engineers.
- **[[37signals]]:** "big things with small teams"; six-week [[Shape Up]] cycles, no PMs.
- **[[Bending Spoons]]:** ~500 staff running a portfolio (Evernote, WeTransfer, Meetup) via autonomous business units.

## The Enabling Inputs

Two preconditions recur: an extreme hiring bar ([[PostHog]]'s "hell yes or no," Bezos's "the bar high in hiring," [[Bending Spoons]]' ~0.04% acceptance rate) and a preference for [[The Generalist|generalists]] who span disciplines.

## The 2025–26 Inflection

AI converts "small team as culture" into "small team as economics." [[Shopify]]'s "prove you can't do it with AI before asking for headcount," [[Anthropic]]'s "hire more PMs, not more engineers" (5 engineers ≈ 15–20 with Claude Code), and record [[Revenue Per Employee]] figures together mark the shift. This is the most direct organizational evidence for the [[The Product Engineer|product-engineer]] thesis.
