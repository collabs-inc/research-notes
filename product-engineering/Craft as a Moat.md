# Craft as a Moat

The thesis that micro-interaction polish, motion, and detail are a *defensible* product advantage — and that they matter more, not less, as AI makes baseline functionality cheap to produce. The [[Craft|craft]] sibling of [[Taste as the Bottleneck]].

## The Exemplars

- **[[Linear]]** — craft as explicit strategy. Karri Saarinen: *"We started with quality… people actually noticed, because it's a rare approach — especially for startups."* No "handoff to dev"; quality is shared, and the sum of "tiny design decisions" determines the product.
- **The Browser Company / Arc** — craft via physics: design engineers borrow from "kinematics, springs, and magnetism"; dragging a tab to split gives a "subtle bounce" that confirms the action.
- **Family** (crypto wallet, Benji Taylor) — principles of simplicity, fluidity, delight; cards animate seamlessly between screens; "Wallet Wrapped" personalized recaps.
- **[[Vercel]]** — craft as institution: the [[Design Engineer]] org plus public artifacts ([[Web Interface Guidelines]], Devouring Details).

## Why It's a Moat

These companies treat craft as go-to-market: [[Linear]] grew largely by word of mouth on the strength of feel. The argument is that the *sum of tiny interaction decisions* is what users feel and what competitors can't cheaply clone — even with AI generating the scaffolding.

## The AI-Era Stakes

When [[Vibe Coding|vibe coding]] makes "good enough" abundant, craft becomes the differentiator. [[Guillermo Rauch]] argues AI can *democratize* polish (embedding best practices into [[v0]]'s output), shifting the human role to curation — knowing which references to feed and what to keep. Craft is increasingly encoded and scaled through [[Design Systems and Velocity|design systems]] and even packaged for agents (e.g. [[Emil Kowalski]]'s "Design Engineer" skill).
